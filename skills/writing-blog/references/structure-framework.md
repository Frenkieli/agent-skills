# Structure Framework

Use this reference when drafting an outline, reorganizing an article, or
diagnosing structural problems.

## Core Principle

A blog article should let readers stop anywhere and still feel that they
understand up to that point. Give the conclusion before evidence and the whole
picture before details.

Do not organize by how the author thought of the idea. Organize by how the
reader can understand it.

## Opening: SCQA

Use SCQA as a thinking framework, not a rigid four-paragraph template:

- **Situation**: start from a familiar scene or accepted fact.
- **Complication**: point out a contradiction, friction, or mismatch.
- **Question**: make the reader understand what question now matters.
- **Answer**: give the central claim.

After the opening, the reader should know what the article is about, why it
matters, and what the author is trying to say.

## Recommended Article Shape

```text
1. Opening
   - Situation -> Complication -> Question -> Answer
   - The central claim appears early.

2. Whole-picture overview
   - The system, argument, workflow, or solution as a whole.
   - A one-sentence summary or analogy.
   - A Mermaid diagram when multiple components, steps, or relationships matter.

3. Core mechanism
   - Two or three key points.
   - For each point: problem -> choice or view -> effect.
   - Order by importance, not chronology.

4. Detail expansion
   - Only details that help the reader understand the core mechanism.
   - Code snippets need context and a reason to exist.

5. Ending
   - Return to the opening question.
   - State the cognitive change, practical value, or final judgment.
```

## Structural Rules

- Section titles or first sentences should summarize each section's conclusion,
  not merely name a topic.
- Each section should answer the question created by the previous section.
- If a section does two unrelated jobs, split it.
- If a term is explained later, do not rely on it earlier.
- When introducing a subtopic, explain how it relates to the whole article.
- Use diagrams for multi-component or multi-step material when pure text would
  force readers to assemble the structure mentally.

## Concept Introduction

When readers first meet a concept, explain:

- what it is in this article
- what role it plays
- why it appears now

A one-sentence explanation is usually enough. Avoid jargon, or explain it in a
parenthesis or subordinate clause on first use.

## Evidence And Argument

- Assertion is not argument. Claims need reasons or evidence.
- Trust, choice, and preference need concrete bases.
- Examples should be self-contained and concrete.
- "Automatic" behavior needs a mechanism.
- Present data, baseline, then interpretation.
